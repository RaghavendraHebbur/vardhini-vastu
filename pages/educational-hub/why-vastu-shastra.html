Modern indoor living has severed many of those natural connections. We work under artificial light, sleep in climate-controlled rooms, eat food prepared far from where we live, and conduct entire careers inside sealed buildings. Every hour spent in a vastu-defective space is an hour of subliminal depletion. Every hour in a vastu-aligned space is an hour of subliminal support. The cumulative effect over years and decades is not trivial.

Modern Construction Ignores Natural Energy

Traditional Indian homes — the courtyard house, the thinnai, the haveli — were built with vastu principles embedded in their design, sometimes without the builder even consciously applying them. Natural light, cross-ventilation, open central courtyards, and directionally appropriate room placement were design defaults of vernacular architecture.

Modern construction has abandoned all of this. Apartment blocks are designed for maximum floor-area-ratio (FAR) — to fit the most units per square metre. Rooms go wherever the structural engineer places them. Kitchens end up in north-east corners because that is where the plumbing stack happens to run. Main doors open in whatever direction faces the corridor. The result: millions of homes with systematic vastu defects that their occupants live with every day, wondering why life feels harder than it should.
